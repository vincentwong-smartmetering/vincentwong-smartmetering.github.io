Executive Summary

Smart water metering has become a key component of utility digitalization strategies across ASEAN. Utilities are investing in smart metering technologies to improve billing accuracy, reduce non-revenue water, enhance operational visibility, and support long-term sustainability goals.

Despite strong interest and increasing investment, many smart water metering projects fail to achieve their intended outcomes. Common issues include unrealistic business cases, inadequate communications coverage, poor vendor selection, insufficient change management, and underestimation of operational complexity.

This whitepaper examines common failure patterns observed in ASEAN smart water metering deployments and provides practical recommendations to improve project success rates.

Key Message: Smart water metering projects rarely fail because of meter technology alone. Most failures originate from planning, governance, communications, operations, and organizational readiness.

Key Findings

1. Technology Is Rarely the Primary Cause of Failure

Utilities often focus heavily on meter specifications while overlooking deployment strategy, data management, operational processes, and user adoption. Successful projects require a complete ecosystem approach.

2. Communications Performance Is Frequently Overestimated

NB-IoT, RF Mesh, LoRaWAN, and other communication technologies each have strengths and limitations. Coverage assumptions that are not validated through field testing can result in significant deployment challenges.

3. Business Objectives Are Often Poorly Defined

Projects should be driven by measurable outcomes such as reduction in non-revenue water, operational efficiency improvements, and customer service enhancement rather than technology adoption alone.

4. Operational Readiness Is Commonly Underestimated

Smart metering introduces new processes involving device management, communications monitoring, firmware updates, analytics, and customer engagement. Utilities must prepare for these operational requirements.

5. Long-Term Data Value Is Often Overlooked

The greatest value of smart metering is often generated after deployment through analytics, leak detection, consumption insights, and operational optimization.
